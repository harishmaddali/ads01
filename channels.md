# Marketing channels for starters

This overview is for people new to digital marketing: what each channel is good for, what you typically need to start, and how to think about fit. You do not need every channel—pick based on your audience, offer, and resources.

## How to choose (quick framework)

1. **Where does your audience already spend time or search for answers?** Meet them there first.
2. **What is your goal?** Awareness needs reach; leads need forms or demos; sales need clear offers and trust.
3. **What can you sustain?** Channels that need fresh content (organic social, SEO) reward consistency; paid channels need budget and monitoring.

---

## Organic search (SEO)

**What it is** — Appearing in non-paid search results when people look for topics related to your product or problem.

**Good for** — Long-term, compounding traffic; high-intent “I am looking for X” demand; education and comparison content.

**To get started** — A crawlable website, clear pages for main topics, helpful content, basic technical hygiene (speed, mobile, indexation), and patience.

**Tradeoffs** — Results take time; competition can be strong; you need measurement (search console, analytics).

---

## Paid search (SEM / search ads)

**What it is** — Bidding on keywords so your text ads appear when people search; you pay per click in most cases.

**Good for** — Capturing existing demand quickly; local services; products people already search for by name or category.

**To get started** — Defined offers, landing pages, conversion tracking, a test budget, and keyword research.

**Tradeoffs** — Costs rise in competitive auctions; poor tracking or weak pages waste spend.

---

## Paid social (Meta, LinkedIn, TikTok, Pinterest, etc.)

**What it is** — Running sponsored posts, video ads, or lead forms inside social platforms.

**Good for** — Awareness, creative testing, reaching interests and lookalikes, some direct response (especially with strong creative and offers).

**To get started** — Business accounts, pixels or events for your site, 2–3 creative angles, clear CTA, and daily/weekly checks while learning.

**Tradeoffs** — Creative fatigue; privacy and tracking changes; each platform has different norms and specs.

---

## Organic social

**What it is** — Unpaid posts, community replies, and profile presence on social networks.

**Good for** — Brand voice, community, customer service, partnerships, and supporting launches—not always reliable reach without ads.

**To get started** — One or two platforms where your audience is real, a simple content rhythm, and guidelines for tone and response time.

**Tradeoffs** — Algorithm reach is limited; consistency matters more than being everywhere.

---

## Email marketing

**What it is** — Newsletters, promotions, and automated sequences to people who opted in (and transactional messages tied to actions).

**Good for** — Retention, repeat purchases, nurturing leads, and owned communication that is not controlled by a platform algorithm.

**To get started** — ESP (email service provider), signup flows, consent and compliance basics, a welcome path, and clear unsubscribe handling.

**Tradeoffs** — List quality beats size; deliverability requires good practices and relevant sends.

---

## Display and programmatic

**What it is** — Banner-style or rich ads across websites and apps, often bought via networks or demand-side platforms.

**Good for** — Reach, remarketing, and supporting other channels with visual reminders.

**To get started** — Creative sizes, tracking, frequency caps, and clear goals (often awareness or assisted conversions rather than last-click only).

**Tradeoffs** — Banner blindness and fraud risk; measurement is less direct than search.

---

## Video (YouTube and in-feed video ads)

**What it is** — Long-form or short video on owned channels or as paid placements before/during/around video content.

**Good for** — Explanation, demos, trust, and broad reach when motion and story matter.

**To get started** — A simple production bar you can repeat, hooks in the first seconds, captions, and links or CTAs matched to the funnel stage.

**Tradeoffs** — Production and iteration cost; skippable formats need strong openings.

---

## Affiliate and partnerships

**What it is** — Paying partners (sites, creators, newsletters) for traffic or sales they drive, usually on performance terms.

**Good for** — Scaling reach with aligned audiences when you have tracking and margin to share.

**To get started** — Clear commission rules, tracking links or codes, partner guidelines, and payout process.

**Tradeoffs** — Brand control and fraud monitoring; needs legal/finance alignment.

---

## Influencer marketing

**What it is** — Collaborating with creators who have an audience that overlaps yours—sponsored posts, bundles, or long-term ambassadors.

**Good for** — Credibility, niche reach, and content you can reuse with agreement.

**To get started** — Criteria for fit (not only follower count), briefs, disclosure compliance, and measurable CTAs where possible.

**Tradeoffs** — Variable quality and reporting; contracts and usage rights matter.

---

## Content marketing (cross-channel)

**What it is** — Blogs, guides, webinars, podcasts, and tools that help your audience; often distributed via SEO, email, and social.

**Good for** — Education, trust, and supporting sales cycles—especially in B2B and considered purchases.

**To get started** — A small number of pillar topics, an editorial calendar you can keep, and promotion plans (not “publish and hope”).

**Tradeoffs** — Requires time and differentiation; measure leading indicators (engagement, leads) not only traffic.

---

## Marketplaces and retail media

**What it is** — Selling on Amazon, app stores, or retailer sites; often includes paid placement *inside* those platforms.

**Good for** — Demand that already exists on the marketplace; physical or digital goods with strong listings.

**To get started** — Optimized listings, reviews strategy within policy, and platform-specific ad tools if you sell there.

**Tradeoffs** — Fees and platform rules; you partially rent the customer relationship.

---

## Events and webinars

**What it is** — Online or in-person sessions for education, demos, and lead capture.

**Good for** — B2B pipeline, launches, and high-touch explanation.

**To get started** — Registration pages, reminders, a follow-up email path, and a single clear next step after the event.

**Tradeoffs** — Operational load; no-show rates on free webinars.

---

## SMS and messaging

**What it is** — Text or chat-app messages to opted-in users (offers, reminders, support).

**Good for** — High open rates for timely messages when the audience expects texts.

**To get started** — Explicit opt-in, quiet hours, easy opt-out, and a provider that supports compliance in your regions.

**Tradeoffs** — Easy to over-message; regulations are strict in many markets.

---

## Recommended starter combinations (examples)

| Situation | Often sensible first steps |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| Local service business | Google Business Profile, local SEO, limited paid search, reviews |
| Ecommerce (broad audience) | Paid social + email + SEO/product pages; remarketing once traffic exists |
| B2B SaaS | Content/SEO + LinkedIn or search + email nurture + webinars |
| Creator or personal brand | One organic social hub + email list + occasional paid boosts |

These are patterns, not rules—validate with small tests and your own numbers.
